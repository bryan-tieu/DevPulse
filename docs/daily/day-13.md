# Day 13 — Gate bronze→silver with Great Expectations: count the quarantine you can't see

> **Phase 2 · Week 5 (back half — quality).** Picks up from Day 12 (the dbt gate is live: `devpulse_ingest` runs 5 tasks ending in `dbt_build`, PASS=69, and the gate's first catch was real — a stray 2026 hour went red on 4 `relationships` tests). But the dbt gate guards **gold only**: it can test nothing that never reached BigQuery. Day 6 banked exactly such a blind spot — malformed-`created_at` rows cast to **NULL** in Spark, fall into `event_date=__HIVE_DEFAULT_PARTITION__`, and the hour-prefix load never sees them. They are **quarantined but uncounted**: silently dropped from the warehouse's point of view, violating "fail loudly, never drop silently" in the one place dbt can't police. Today a **Great Expectations gate between the Spark transform and the BQ load** validates the silver Parquet and **counts the quarantine**, failing the run when rows went missing.
>
> **Scope call (read first):** IN — a `quality/` GE stack (own image, Day 8 rule), an expectation suite over the hour's silver Parquet, the counted quarantine + raw-vs-silver reconciliation, a `validate_silver` DockerOperator between `silver_transform` and `load_silver`, and red+green proof. OUT — **failure alerting and run-metadata logging → Day 14** (the old status bundled three lessons; split at planning per one-day-one-lesson — Day 14 will consume today's validation-result artifact). Also OUT: GE data docs site (generate once locally if curious, don't wire it), GE's Spark/BQ execution engines (named as the at-scale step, not used), GE on any other layer boundary.
>
> **The day's central decision, stated up front and honestly:** *why GE, and why here?* More dbt tests can't do this job — dbt only sees loaded rows. Asserts inside the Spark job could, but they entangle validation with transform and emit no standard artifact (at scale, in-flight GE-on-Spark is a real option — named, not used). Airflow's SQL check operators only work post-load. GE gives **declarative expectations + a machine-readable Validation Result** — the exact artifact Day 14's alerting and run-metadata will consume. Engine honesty: we run GE's **pandas engine over one hour** (180k rows, trivial); at scale you'd swap to the Spark or BQ execution engine and the *suite survives the swap* — same contract, different transport, rhyming with Day 12's DockerOperator→K8s answer. And the day's intellectual core, stated plainly: **`expect_column_values_to_not_be_null(created_at)` on the hour partition is vacuously green** — the malformed rows aren't *in* the partition being inspected. A gate over what survived can't see what fell out. You must **count what's missing, not inspect what's present** — that's what "formalize the quarantine" means.

## Learning goals
1. **Every layer boundary needs its own gate** — dbt tests can only interrogate rows that reached the warehouse; data dropped *before* load is structurally invisible to them, which is why quality tooling exists at the lake boundary and not just in the modeling layer.
2. **Vacuous checks, and counting what's missing** — a `not_null` over the surviving partition is green *by construction* when the nulls were routed elsewhere; formalizing a quarantine means counting the quarantine, and a reconciliation identity (raw = loaded + deduped + quarantined) is what "no silent drops" looks like as an equation.
3. **The quarantine attribution and lifecycle problem** — when the scoping field is the broken field, the quarantine can't be per-run (so the check is global), and under dynamic partition overwrite it is never cleaned automatically (a partition absent from new data is never rewritten) — quarantines need owned lifecycle, not just existence.
4. **GE's anatomy and why the artifact matters** — expectations/suites/checkpoints produce a machine-readable Validation Result, versus hand-rolled asserts that produce a stack trace; the standard artifact is what alerting, run-metadata, and data docs can all consume without re-instrumenting.
5. **Gate placement economics** — Day 12's gate caught bad data *after* it reached gold (three layers of cleanup, merge can't un-merge); today's catches it *before* the warehouse write (cleanup = delete one fixture); the earlier the gate, the cheaper the failure.
6. **Thresholds versus exact assertions** — volume bounds and a residual tolerance encode "normal" without brittle magic numbers, while identities (every row accounted for) stay exact; knowing which check gets which treatment is the DQ craft.
7. **The engine swap at scale** — pandas over one hour is honest here; GE's Spark and BigQuery execution engines run the *same suite* in-flight or in-warehouse — the suite is the contract, the engine is transport, the same shape as Day 12's DockerOperator→K8s answer.
