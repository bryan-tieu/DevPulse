And the metadata table is written by a task *inside* the DAG it observes вЂ” honest for one DAG; at scale observability moves out-of-band (listeners/OpenLineage) so the observer can't die with the patient.

## Learning goals
1. **Retry policy is a routing decision, not a number** вЂ” retries exist for stochastic failures (infra flakes made free by idempotency); data failures are deterministic, so retrying them only delays the page вЂ” being able to argue `retries=0` *as the correct setting* for a gate is the interview answer.
2. **Callbacks compose with retries** вЂ” `on_failure_callback` fires only after the retry budget exhausts, which is exactly what makes "retry infra silently, page data immediately" expressible with two knobs.
3. **Logs are for humans, artifacts are for machines** вЂ” parsing log lines couples consumers to formatting; a structured summary derived from the same verdict booleans gives every consumer (operator, alert, metadata table, Day 15+ API) one source of truth.
4. **`trigger_rule="all_done"` and the observer problem** вЂ” the default `all_success` is why gates stop pipelines, and the override is why observers must opt out of being stopped; a metadata logger that dies with the failure it should record is worse than none.
5. **Free load jobs vs streaming inserts** вЂ” the same cost discipline as Day 7's load-not-query decision, applied to telemetry; at this scale the pennies don't matter but the habit does.
6. **Page fatigue as a first-class design constraint** вЂ” alerting on every retry trains humans to ignore alerts; the scarcest resource in an alerting system is the recipient's trust.
7. **In-band vs out-of-band observability** вЂ” a task inside the DAG can record that DAG honestly, until the scheduler itself is the failure; the production shape (listeners, OpenLineage, external observers) survives what it observes.
